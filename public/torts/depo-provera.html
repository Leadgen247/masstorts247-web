AWhat Is This Tort

Depo-Provera is Pfizer's brand name for depot medroxyprogesterone acetate (DMPA), a long-acting injectable progestin contraceptive administered intramuscularly every 12–13 weeks. It is one of the most widely used contraceptives in the United States, with an estimated 1–2 million women on it at any given time and roughly 74 million users worldwide.

Injury Alleged

Plaintiffs allege that prolonged use of Depo-Provera causes intracranial meningiomas — tumors that arise from the meninges, the protective membranes covering the brain and spinal cord. Meningiomas are usually histologically benign but can be clinically devastating: depending on location, they cause persistent headache, vision loss and papilledema, seizures, hearing loss, personality changes, and cognitive decline. Treatment typically requires craniotomy and long-term neurological follow-up. Medical treatment costs can exceed $700,000 per patient before insurance offsets.

Who Is Affected

The MDL's eligibility criteria, narrowed by Judge Rodgers, require plaintiffs to document: (1) at least two Depo-Provera injections, (2) a diagnosed intracranial meningioma, and (3) no prior brain tumor history. Typical plaintiff profile: woman diagnosed in her 30s–50s after 2+ years of continuous use. Risk increases with cumulative dose; the French BMJ 2024 study reported a 5.6× risk at ≥1 year of use.

Product & Substance

Depot medroxyprogesterone acetate, a synthetic progestin. Marketed as Depo-Provera (150 mg IM every 3 months) and Depo-SubQ Provera 104 (104 mg SC every 3 months). Generic and authorized-generic DMPA distributed by Pharmacia & Upjohn, Greenstone LLC, Viatris, and Prasco Laboratories — all named co-defendants.

Category: Drug · Pharmaceutical  ·  Legal theory: Product liability — failure to warn, design defect, negligent misrepresentation.

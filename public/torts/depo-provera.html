AWhat Is This Tort

Depo-Provera is Pfizer's brand name for depot medroxyprogesterone acetate (DMPA), a long-acting injectable progestin contraceptive administered intramuscularly every 12–13 weeks. It is one of the most widely used contraceptives in the United States, with an estimated 1–2 million women on it at any given time and roughly 74 million users worldwide.

Injury Alleged

Plaintiffs allege that prolonged use of Depo-Provera causes intracranial meningiomas — tumors that arise from the meninges, the protective membranes covering the brain and spinal cord. Meningiomas are usually histologically benign but can be clinically devastating: depending on location, they cause persistent headache, vision loss and papilledema, seizures, hearing loss, personality changes, and cognitive decline. Treatment typically requires craniotomy and long-term neurological follow-up. Medical treatment costs can exceed $700,000 per patient before insurance offsets.

Who Is Affected

The MDL's eligibility criteria, narrowed by Judge Rodgers, require plaintiffs to document: (1) at least two Depo-Provera injections, (2) a diagnosed intracranial meningioma, and (3) no prior brain tumor history. Risk rises with cumulative dose: the French BMJ cohort observed significantly increased risk beginning at approximately 12 months of use, with the steepest elevation at 5+ years of exposure. Meningiomas are more common in women over 45, and approximately two-thirds of the meningioma patients in the French cohort were in that age group.

Product & Substance

The active pharmaceutical ingredient is medroxyprogesterone acetate, a synthetic progestin. Depo-Provera is the 150 mg intramuscular formulation; Pfizer also markets a 104 mg subcutaneous version (Depo-SubQ Provera 104) — plaintiffs contend the SubQ formulation is a safer alternative because of its lower dose and subcutaneous route, a point the JPML itself noted in its consolidation order. Generic DMPA is manufactured by Greenstone (Pfizer's authorized generic), Prasco, and Viatris.

The Depo-Provera mass tort formed unusually quickly compared with most pharmaceutical MDLs — roughly 10 months from the foundational epidemiological study to JPML consolidation. The accelerant was a March 2024 study in the British Medical Journal by researchers at France's EPI-PHARE (the national agency for the safety of medicines), which reported a 5.6× increased risk of meningioma requiring surgery among women who had used injectable medroxyprogesterone acetate for more than one year.

French regulators and Canadian health authorities moved quickly: both updated their product labels in 2024 to warn about meningioma. The U.S. response was asymmetric. Pfizer submitted a proposed U.S. label change to the FDA in 2024; the FDA rejected it. Plaintiffs' attorneys allege the proposed language was deliberately vague and scientifically thin — a strategic choice designed to produce a rejection Pfizer could later weaponize as a federal preemption shield.

In November 2024, plaintiff counsel petitioned the JPML to consolidate an initial 22 federal cases. On February 7, 2025, the JPML issued its consolidation order and assigned the MDL to Judge M. Casey Rodgers in the Northern District of Florida — the same judge who managed MDL 2885 (3M Combat Arms Earplugs), one of the largest mass torts in U.S. history. By April 2026 the MDL had grown to 3,490+ plaintiffs, with plaintiff firms reporting another ~10,000 unfiled claims in inventory awaiting proof-of-use verification.

In December 2025 — mid-MDL and with Pfizer's preemption motion pending — the FDA reversed its prior position and added a meningioma warning to the U.S. Depo-Provera label. This development significantly complicates Pfizer's preemption defense.

CCausation Theory

Mechanism of harm. Meningioma cells express progesterone receptors at elevated density. Medroxyprogesterone acetate is a potent synthetic progestin that binds and stimulates those receptors, driving tumor cell proliferation. The relationship is dose-dependent and duration-dependent: risk rises meaningfully beginning around 12 months of cumulative exposure and continues to climb with longer use, consistent with a sustained mitogenic stimulus rather than a one-time toxic event.

Epidemiological support. Two well-powered modern studies now anchor the causation theory. Roland et al. (BMJ 2024) used French national health records to analyze more than 18,000 women who underwent meningioma surgery between 2009 and 2018 and found a 5.6× relative risk associated with DMPA use of one year or longer. Xiao et al. (JAMA Neurology 2025), drawing on 10.4 million U.S. women across 68 health systems, reported a propensity-matched relative risk of 2.43 (95% CI 1.77–3.33) with a number-needed-to-harm of 1,152. Plaintiffs will rely on both to establish general causation at the June 2026 hearings.

Corporate knowledge and conduct. Discovery is expected to surface Pfizer's internal pharmacovigilance data on meningioma case reports dating back years. Of particular significance: Pfizer and its New Zealand subsidiary updated the Depo-Provera data sheet in New Zealand in February 2024 to warn about meningioma, and Canadian and European regulators received stronger warnings than U.S. patients. Plaintiffs will argue this regulatory asymmetry demonstrates that Pfizer knew and could have strengthened the U.S. label under the FDA's Changes Being Effected (CBE) regulation without prior agency approval.

Failure to warn. The operative claim is not that Depo-Provera is unreasonably dangerous per se, but that Pfizer failed to warn U.S. prescribers and patients of a known and serious risk — a risk Pfizer disclosed to its own regulators abroad. The existence of a clearly safer alternative within Pfizer's own portfolio (Depo-SubQ Provera 104 at 104 mg subcutaneous) sharpens the failure-to-warn theory into a "reasonable alternative design" argument where state law permits it.

DLitigation Status

The MDL is in its general causation phase. The single highest-stakes question in the litigation right now is Pfizer's federal preemption motion for summary judgment, argued on September 29, 2025 — the ruling is expected in 2026 and will determine whether the MDL proceeds to bellwether trials or faces wholesale dismissal.

Pilot (Bellwether) Cases

Judge Rodgers has selected five pilot cases to proceed to trial first: Toney, Blonski, Schmidt, Wilson, and Arceo v. Pfizer. The first bellwether, Toney v. Pfizer, is scheduled for December 7, 2026. Plaintiff verdicts in early bellwethers historically drive settlement matrices; a defense verdict would recalibrate case values dramatically in the opposite direction.

Central Legal Dispute — Federal Preemption

Pfizer argues that because the FDA rejected its proposed meningioma warning in 2024, Wyeth v. Levine–style CBE amendments are foreclosed and state failure-to-warn claims are impliedly preempted. Plaintiffs counter that (a) the proposed label was so imprecise it effectively invited rejection, (b) Pfizer's own New Zealand data sheet and Canadian labeling prove stronger warnings were scientifically defensible, and (c) the FDA's December 2025 reversal vitiates the preemption argument on its own terms. A ruling for Pfizer would collapse most of the MDL. A ruling for plaintiffs accelerates everything.

⚠ State Court Activity — Parallel Track

New York: Approximately 78 plaintiffs consolidated before Judge Sabrina Kraus (Sup. Ct., N.Y. County), with BrownGreer assisting in case verification. Delaware: Active coordination with the MDL schedule. Judge Rodgers held a joint case management conference on October 27, 2025, aligning causation deadlines across jurisdictions.

For firms filing outside the MDL, state court remains a live option — particularly in NY where the revival statutes and discovery rule are favorable.

HStatute of Limitations

MPubMed — Peer-Reviewed Literature

BMJ 2024 · DOI: 10.1136/bmj-2023-078078
Use of progestogens and the risk of intracranial meningioma: national case-control study
Roland N, Neumann A, Hoisnard L, et al. — EPI-PHARE (French National Agency for Medicines Safety)
The foundational study. Analyzed French national health records for 18,061 women who underwent meningioma surgery (2009–2018), matched against 90,305 population controls. Identified a 5.55× odds ratio (95% CI 3.43–8.99) for women with ≥1 year of DMPA exposure. Dose-response relationship observed; no elevated risk below one year of use. Concluded the signal is "sufficient to justify a warning."
"Use of medroxyprogesterone acetate for more than one year was associated with a 5.6-fold increased risk of intracranial meningioma requiring surgery."
PMID: 38537944 — Expert witness material for general causation
JAMA Neurology 2025 · DOI: 10.1001/jamaneurol.2025.3011
Depot Medroxyprogesterone Acetate and Risk of Meningioma in the US
Xiao T, Kumar P, Lobbous M, et al. — Cleveland Clinic / Case Western Reserve
First large U.S. replication. Retrospective cohort of 10,425,438 women across 68 U.S. health systems via TriNetX (Dec 2004–Dec 2024). After propensity-score matching (88,667 DMPA users vs. controls), found RR 2.43 (95% CI 1.77–3.33) for meningioma diagnosis. Number-needed-to-harm: 1,152 for DMPA vs. 3,020 for oral MPA. No elevated risk for combined oral contraceptives, progestin-only pills, LNG-IUDs, or etonogestrel implants.
"DMPA use was associated with a significantly increased risk of meningioma diagnosis in a large U.S. population, while other hormonal contraceptive methods were not."
PMID: 40892397 — Anchors U.S. general causation argument
Current Oncology 2025 · DOI: 10.3390/curroncol32070401
A Matched Case-Control Study Examining the Association Between Exposure to DMPA and Cerebral Meningioma Using an Active Comparator
Griffin R, Arend R — University of Alabama at Birmingham
Single-center case-control (UAB, 2015–2024) using active-comparator design (meningioma cases vs. women with other tumors: breast, skin, non-meningioma brain).
